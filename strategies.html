Strategy Bible

The study library for TOEFL ITP improvement.

This page is your study backbone: the place where you learn what to do before, during, and after practice. The goal is not memorizing tricks. The goal is recognizing patterns with calm and knowing exactly why an answer is right.

Listening Structure Reading

How to use this library

Study the strategy, practice it, then review your mistake.

1. Name the question type.

If you do not know what the question is testing, you answer by feeling. Naming the type gives you control.

2. Use one strategy at a time.

A short drill should focus on one skill: agreement, inference, tone, reference, passive voice, or main idea.

3. Review the exact trap.

The most useful feedback is not just correct or incorrect. It explains the distractor and the thinking error.

L

Listening Comprehension

Listen for meaning, attitude, and organization.

Listening is not about catching every word. It is about knowing what matters: who is speaking, what changed, what the speaker really means, and how the ideas are organized.

Before audio

Preview the answer choices quickly and predict the topic or question focus.

During audio

Track meaning, speaker attitude, contrast words, reasons, and final decisions.

After audio

Eliminate repeated-word traps and choose the option that matches the idea.

Second speaker priority

In short conversations, the second speaker often gives the real answer: refusal, correction, opinion, or problem.

Paraphrase, not repetition

Distractors often repeat words from the audio. Correct answers often restate the meaning with different words.

Negative meaning

Train your ear to catch not, hardly, barely, seldom, neither, nor, impossible, fail to, and unlikely.

Double negatives

Not uncommon, cannot deny, hardly impossible, and similar forms may create a positive meaning.

Idiomatic responses

Expressions such as I wish I could, You can say that again, Beats me, and It slipped my mind carry implied meaning.

Speaker attitude

Listen for surprise, doubt, disappointment, approval, hesitation, relief, and disagreement through tone and wording.

Function questions

Ask why the speaker says something: to suggest, complain, clarify, refuse, correct, or ask for help.

Problem and solution

Many campus conversations follow this pattern: situation, problem, suggestion, decision.

Contrast markers

But, however, actually, instead, though, and on the other hand often signal the answer.

Cause and result

Because, due to, since, therefore, so, and as a result explain why something happened.

Sequence language

First, next, finally, before, after, and meanwhile help you follow lectures and longer conversations.

Lecture topic map

For talks, write a mental outline: topic, definition, example, contrast, result, conclusion.

Detail discipline

Do not chase every number or name. Details matter when connected to purpose, example, cause, or contrast.

Prediction from options

If options include locations, listen for where. If they include reasons, listen for why.

Move on quickly

If you miss one item, do not emotionally carry that mistake into the next audio.

Listening practice rule

Practice should alternate between slow strategy work and timed listening. Strategy first, pressure later.

S

Structure and Written Expression

Find the grammar pattern before choosing an answer.

Structure is where you gain points by becoming systematic. The first job is not choosing an answer. The first job is finding the missing grammar relationship.

Step 1

Find the subject, verb, connector, and missing part before looking at choices.

Step 2

Classify the trap: agreement, tense, form, clause, preposition, or word order.

Step 3

Eliminate options that break the required structure, not options that merely sound strange.

Open full Structure strategy library 20 strategies
Subject-verb agreement

Ignore phrases between the subject and verb. Each, every, the number of, and gerund subjects are singular.

Prepositional interruptions

Of the participants, in the laboratory, with assistants, and along with the team do not change the real subject.

Correlative agreement

With either...or and neither...nor, the verb often agrees with the nearer subject.

Verb tense signals

Since and in recent years often point to present perfect. Before another past action may point to past perfect.

Passive voice

If the subject receives the action, use be + past participle. With modals: must be done, should be reviewed.

Subjunctive

After suggest, recommend, insist, essential, and important that, use the base form: that he be, that she go.

Adjective clauses

Who is for people as subjects, whom is for objects, whose shows possession, which is for things.

Noun clauses

Embedded questions use statement word order: what the cause was, not what was the cause.

Reduced clauses

Active reductions often use -ing. Passive reductions often use past participles.

Participles

Use -ing when the noun does the action. Use -ed forms when the noun receives or experiences the action.

Gerunds and infinitives

Some verbs take gerunds, some take infinitives, and prepositions are followed by gerunds.

Preposition patterns

Depend on, consist of, interested in, responsible for, object to, and succeed in must be learned as patterns.

Parallel structure

Items in a list must match: noun with noun, gerund with gerund, infinitive with infinitive.

Comparatives

Use than after comparatives. Watch more, less, fewer, as...as, the same as, and different from.

Inversions

After negative adverbials like never, rarely, seldom, and only after, use auxiliary + subject + verb.

Connectors

Although and because need a clause. Despite and because of need a noun phrase.

Word forms

Check if the sentence needs a noun, verb, adjective, or adverb. Endings often reveal the answer.

Articles and determiners

Watch singular count nouns. A singular count noun usually needs a determiner such as a, the, this, or each.

Pronoun reference

Pronouns must match number, person, and meaning. They, it, this, and those must point clearly to a noun.

Error identification routine

For underlined parts, classify each option. Do not reread randomly. Check verb, agreement, form, connector, preposition.

Structure practice rule

Guided practice should be untimed. Timing belongs in mock exams. First build accuracy and pattern recognition.

R

Reading Comprehension

Read with a mission, not with panic.

Reading is not a vocabulary contest. You need to locate information, understand relationships, and avoid answer choices that are too broad, too narrow, too extreme, or not supported.

Before questions

Skim title, first lines, topic shifts, and paragraph purpose.

During questions

Name the task: main idea, detail, inference, vocabulary, reference, or organization.

After choosing

Find proof in the passage. If there is no textual support, the answer is risky.

Open full Reading strategy library 15 strategies
Main idea

Read the beginning and ending. Eliminate choices that focus on one detail instead of the whole passage.

Primary purpose

Ask what the author is doing: explaining, comparing, criticizing, describing, proposing, or presenting evidence.

Detail questions

Use keywords to locate the sentence. Do not answer from memory when the text is still available.

Negative detail

For NOT and EXCEPT, mark the three true choices first. The correct answer is the one not supported.

Vocabulary in context

Do not use only dictionary meaning. Read the sentence before and after to find the local meaning.

Pronoun reference

Look backward. Match singular/plural and meaning before choosing what it, they, which, or this refers to.

Inference

The answer must be suggested by the text, not by personal knowledge. Avoid extreme language.

Author attitude

Notice words that show approval, doubt, criticism, caution, importance, or uncertainty.

Rhetorical function

Ask why a detail is mentioned: as an example, contrast, definition, result, problem, or evidence.

Paragraph role

Each paragraph usually has a job: introduce topic, explain process, give example, show contrast, or conclude.

Transition words

However, moreover, for example, therefore, in contrast, and consequently show how ideas connect.

Cause and effect

Track what caused what. Many wrong answers reverse the relationship or confuse cause with result.

Comparison

Notice similarities and differences. Watch both, unlike, whereas, more than, less than, and in contrast.

Sentence simplification

Find the core idea first. Ignore extra clauses, examples, and modifiers until the main message is clear.

Time management

Do not read every passage slowly first. Use question type to decide when to scan and when to read carefully.

Reading practice rule

Prove your answers with a line or sentence from the passage. No proof, no confidence.
